SCA Score
Specialty Coffee Association cupping scale (0-100). Scores of 80+ are "specialty" grade. POPX targets 85+ for all single-origins.
Q Grader
SCA-certified professional taster. Undergoes rigorous training in sensory evaluation. POPX trusts Q Grader certifications for quality assessment.
Cupping
Standardized tasting protocol. Grounds steeped 4 minutes, then slurped loudly to spray palate. Allows objective flavor comparison across coffees.
Body
Mouthfeel weight‚Äîhow "heavy" or "light" coffee feels. From light tea-like to heavy syrupy. Determined by processing and roast.
Acidity
Bright, tangy sensation (not sour). Creates liveliness and complexity. Altitude and variety heavily influence acidity profiles.
Sweetness
Residual sugars and pleasant flavor complexity. Well-developed sweetness is mark of quality‚Äîoften fruit-like or caramel-like.
Aftertaste
Lingering flavor after swallowing. Long, complex aftertaste indicates quality and balance. Should be pleasant, never bitter.
Mouthfeel
Textural sensation in mouth‚Äîcreamy, smooth, velvety, dry, etc. Key component of overall coffee experience.
Terroir
French concept: soil, climate, altitude, culture combine to create unique flavor expression. Coffee terroir is as complex as wine.
Microclimate
Specific local climate within larger region. Elevation changes of 100m can create distinct flavor profiles in same origin.
Altitude
Elevation of farm (meters above sea level). Higher altitude = slower ripening = more complex sugars = better specialty coffee (usually 1500m+).
Fermentation
Microbial breakdown of sugars and pectin during processing. Creates flavor complexity. Can be wild or controlled (anaerobic).
Mucilage
Sticky fruit layer between skin and parchment. Removed in washed process, kept in honey/natural processes. Affects sweetness and body.
Parchment
Protective layer between mucilage and bean. Removed in milling. Coffee is shipped as "green beans" in parchment.
Green Bean
Unroasted coffee seed. Usually gray-green color. Raw state before roasting develops flavors.
Roast Profile
Temperature curve during roasting. Light/Medium/Dark roasts refer to development time and resulting color/flavor intensity.
First Crack
Audible pop when coffee hits ~200¬∞C. Water escapes, beans expand. Marks transition from light to medium roasts.
Second Crack
Second audible pop at ~225¬∞C. Cell walls break further. Marks transition to dark roasts. Beyond this point, bitter compounds increase.
Degassing
Release of CO2 after roasting (1-5 days typical). Freshly roasted coffee is not ideal for espresso immediately‚Äîneeds to rest.
Extraction
Process of dissolving soluble coffee compounds into water. Optimal extraction ~18-22% of dry bean weight. Determines strength and flavor.
TDS (Total Dissolved Solids)
Percentage of dissolved coffee in water (measured with refractometer). 1.2-1.5% indicates espresso; 1.15-1.35% for pour-over.
Bloom
First step of brewing: hot water poured over grounds causing them to "bloom" (swell from CO2 release). 30-45 seconds typical.
Pour Over
Manual brewing method pouring hot water through grounds in cone/dripper. V60, Chemex, Melitta popular styles. 3-4 minute brew.
V60
Japanese pour-over dripper with spiral ridges. Shallow angle design emphasizes pour control and flavor clarity.
Aeropress
Immersion brewing: grounds steeped 1-2 minutes then pressed through micro-filter. Creates rich cup with clarity.
Cold Brew
Long immersion (12-24 hours) in cold water. Creates smooth, sweet concentrate with lower acidity and caffeine clarity.
Espresso
Pressurized extraction (9+ bars). Finely ground coffee, hot water forced through at high pressure. Creates concentrated shot.
